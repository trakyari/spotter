Resumes

The Difference.

A resume is going to be your one and only chance to capture a potential employer's attention, or for your resume to be tossed into the file of those they don't want to pursue. The statistics are that the average employer only spends a few seconds looking at a resume, so it is imperative that it makes an impression. TheLadders, an online job-matching service found that recruiters only spent an average of 6 seconds on an individual resume, compared to the several minutes that was thought before. Therefore, your resume has to sell both you, your skills, abilities, and accomplishments.

"recruiters only spend an average of 6 seconds on an individual resume"

The Components Of A Resume

Think of your resume this way: It’s an advertisement, and YOU are the product. Your goal is to get hiring managers to buy into what you’re selling – which means giving you an interview. To accomplish that, you need to see it as your marketing tool.

A cover letter allows you to target the job and the employer in a very specific way, leaving the resume to market your skills, qualities and experience as a part of the bigger picture. Your resume then needs less tweaking with each application because the letter, which must be different each time, does that for you.

Resume

A resume is often 1 or 2 pages long and follows a basic format:

  • One’s name
  • A summary
  • Experience
  • Education
  • Other skills, such as fluency in different languages.
Cover Letter

On a cover letter one should always:

  • Don't just rehash your resume — be strategic
  • Tailor it to a specific job
  • Address the hiring manager personally
  • Use standard business letter format
  • Use keywords from the job description, if available
